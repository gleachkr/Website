David Hume: The Origins of Ideas

Where we are

The ideas of substance, the self, and God, are genuine and meaningful.

But they cannot be found in experience.

Therefore, not all genuinely meaningful ideas have their origins in experience.

Ok, then, where do they come from?

Descartes suggests that they were ‘impressed upon us’. But that just deepens the mystery.

The Humean Account

Impressions

Occurent sensations, feelings, inward and outward (the feelings of judging or desiring, for example).

Ideas

The less “forcible” perceptions that we have when we imagine things or think of them.

Basic claim

Ideas are copies and recombinations of Impressions.

Support

  1. Examples
  2. Ability to form certain ideas (of colors, of sounds…) seems to depend on prior impressions.

Possible counterexamples?

  1. The Missing Blue.
  2. General ideas
  3. Substance, God, etc.

Inversion of the Cartesian Argument

Some ideas (e.g. the idea of substance) cannot be found in experience

But but all genuinely meaningful ideas have their origins in experience.

So these ideas are not genuinely meaningful

Humean Criterion

If you suspect that a term (especially in philosophy) is being used without any real idea behind it, ask from what impression is the supposed idea derived. If you find nothing, then the term is most likely an empty word.

Cause and Effect

Relations of Ideas vs. Matters of Fact

What’s the idea here?

Two different ways of inferring, going beyond what you currently know.

Causal Inference

From cause to effect, from effect to cause.

How do we know about these causal relations?

Humean claim: Constant Conjunction. One thing (almost) always follows the other.

Argument

Could you know how an entirely novel kind of object would behave, in a totally new situation?

Isn’t almost any kind of behavior conceivable? If a behavior x is conceivable, how can we know that the object won’t do x?

The connection between cause and effect is arbitrary

Hence, it can’t be known in advance. Only by experience.

The Foundations of Causal Inference

What justifies the inference from the fact that one thing has always followed another in the past, to the claim that that thing will always follow the other in the future?

Regularity of Nature

The way things will behave resembles the way that they have behaved in the past.

What justifies our belief in the regularity of nature?

It’s conceivable that it could be otherwise. So it is a matter of fact, not a relation of ideas.

But, then it seems that the regularity of nature needs to be used to justify our belief in the regularity of nature…
