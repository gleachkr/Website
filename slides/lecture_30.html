Descartes and Doubt

Descartes (1596-1650)

  1. Jesuit Education at La Flèche
  2. Military Enlistment at 22
  3. (1619-1628) Leaves Military, befriends Mersenne, travels.
  4. 1628-1633: Settles in Amsterdam. Writes The Regulae, The World.
  5. 1637: Divides The World, publishes parts along with the discourse on method.
  6. 1640: The Meditations

Scientific Contributions

  1. The Geometry: application of algebra to geometry.
  2. Physics: three laws of motion (motion is along straight lines, things remain in the same state unless acted upon by an outside force, “quantity of motion” is conserved and transfered in collision)
  3. Medicine: mechanistic conception of biology
  4. Optics: Snell’s law
  5. Meteorology, Cosmology…

The Method

Perspective

the diversity of our opinions does not arise from some being endowed with a larger share of reason than others, but solely from this, that we conduct our thoughts along different ways, and do not fix our attention on the same objects.

Revelation

Our epistemological situation is dire.

[upon completing my education] I found myself involved in so many doubts and errors, that I was convinced I had advanced no farther in all my attempts at learning, than the discovery at every turn of my own ignorance… I knew the judgement others had formed of me; and I did not find that I was considered inferior to my fellows… I was thus led to the liberty of judging all men as myself.

Foundations

I compared the disquisitions of the ancient Moralists to very towering and magnificent palaces with no better foundation than sand and mud.

As to the other Sciences, inasmuch as these borrow their principles from philosophy, I judged that no solid superstructures could be reared on foundations so infirm

The Book of the World

I should find more truth in the reasonings of each individual with reference to the affairs in which he is personally interested, and the issue of which must presently punish him if he is judged amiss, than in those conducted by a man of letters in his study

A Single Design

there is seldom so much perfection in works composed of many separate parts, upon which different hands have been employed, as in those completed by a single master

Rebuilding

as for the opinions which up to that time I had embraced, I thought that I could not do better than resolve at once to sweep them wholly away.

The Method

  1. More productive than logic (which serves only to convince)
  2. More general than geometry (which concerns only figures)
  3. More certain than algebra (which is frankly, quite obscure)

Rules

  1. Only accept that which we cannot find grounds to doubt.
  2. Divide problems into parts.
  3. Start with simple things, and move in small steps.
  4. Take care not to omit any possibility.

all things, to the knowledge of which man is competent, are mutually connected in the same way, and that there is nothing so far removed from us as to be beyond our reach, or so hidden that we cannot discover it, provided only we abstain from accepting the false for the true, and always preserve in our thoughts the order necessary for the deduction of one truth from another.

Similarities and differences to/from Bacon?

Baconian Empericism

We found scientific knowledge not on axioms or certain truths, but on a large number of repeatable observations. A broad basis addresses the failure of scholastic inquiry.

Cartesian Rationalism

We found scientific knowledge on absolutely certain axioms, and absolutely certain deductions. A renewed attention to rigour addresses the failure of scholastic inquiry.

Principle of Doubt

Only Accept that which we cannot find grounds to doubt.

If, when we consider a belief b, there is a doubt, d, such that d cannot be true at the same time as b, and we cannot find solid grounds (not depending on b) for rejecting d, then we must not accept b.

Example:

Doubt: Perhaps my vision is deceiving me.

Grounds for rejecting b in most cases: our vision is reliable in most cases (it is corroborated by our other senses)

Doubt: Perhaps I am dreaming.
