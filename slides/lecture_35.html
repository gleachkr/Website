David Hume: Against “Oneself”

Where we are

  1. Every legitimate idea has its origins in experience
  2. In experience, we encounter successive impressions, but we are never directly aware of a necessary connection between one experience and another.

Hence, the idea of a necessary connection between ideas is empty.

Beliefs are of two kinds: those expressing a relation of ideas, and those expressing a matter of fact.

We know about relations of ideas by proof—unpacking definitions, and using logic inferences.

We know about matters of fact that we cannot directly observe by causal reasoning: infering the presence of a cause from its effects, and vice versa.

How do we know that causal relations are present?

Not by observing a “necessary connection” between cause and effect.

Must be by repeated observation.

How do we know repeatedly observed connections will reoccur?

Uniformity of nature.

How do we know nature is uniform? Not by proving it, since the uniformity of nature is a matter of fact, not a relation of ideas.

The universe has been pretty uniform up until now…

But we can’t use that observation to prove the uniformity of nature, without presupposing the uniformity of nature…

The Skeptical Solution

Belief in the uniformity of nature is not supported by anything

Or at least not by evidence of any kind.

Instead, it is mere custom that brings us to believe in it.

Custom

The cause of acts an beliefs which are reinforced by repetition without any correlative reasoning or process of understanding.

The Humean Model

  1. Find an alleged concept which cannot be analyzed as a copy or recombination of direct impressions in experience.
  2. Separate out
    1. The conditions of experience that lead us to apply this concept.
    2. The drives that explain our application in these conditions.
  3. Use (2) to present a picture in which we have no need to postulate something that the alleged concept actually refers to or expresses.

How does he do this in the case of causation?

Does explaining why someone believes something, in terms that have nothing to do with the reasons for belief, show the belief to be unjustified?

The Bundle Theory

We are not substances.

We are bundles of experiences, whose parts come and go.

The question of our identity over time is analogous to the question of whether material things persist through changes in composition. Like that question, it has no good answer.
