Bacon

  • Contemporary of Galileo: Lived 1561-1626, in England
  • Educated in the renaissance humanistic tradition, critical of scholasticism from an early age.
  • A legislator for most of his professional life; parliament, and attorney general.

In Philosophy: Systematic criticism of Aristotle, Scholasticism. Search for

  1. an alternative model of discovery
  2. alternative organizational scheme for knowledge.

The Problem

the primary notions of things which the mind readily and passively imbibes, stores up, and accumulates (and it is from them that all the rest flow) are false, confused, and overhastily abstracted from the facts; nor are the second and subsequent notions less arbitrary and inconsistent

From which it follows that the entire fabric of human reason which we employ in the inquisition of nature is badly put together and built up, like some magnificent structure without any foundation.

The Solution

there was but one course left, therefore,—to try the whole thing anew upon a better plan, and to commence a total reconstruction of sciences, arts, and all human knowledge, raised upon the proper foundations.

when men sailed only by observation of stars, they could indeed coast along the shores of the old continent or cross a few and small Mediterranean seas; but before the ocean could be traversed, the use of the mariners needle… had to be found out… before we can reach the remoter and more hidden parts of nature, it is necessary that a more perfect use and application of the human mind and intellect be introduced.

Doctrine of the Idols

Some can be cast out. Some we can only mark and reprove.

Idols of the Tribe

Intrinsic faults of the human understanding.

It is a false assertion that the sense of man is the measure of things. On the contrary, all perceptions, as well as the sense of the mind, are according to the measure of the individual and not according to the measure of the universe.

Idols of the Cave

Our personal biases and obsessions

  1. Side effects of our education or background
  2. The tendency to explain everything in terms of our favorite metaphor or model.

let every student of nature take this as a rule,—that whatever his mind seizes and dwells upon with peculiar satisfaction is to be held in suspicion.

Idols of the Marketplace

Confusions resulting from the interactions of human beings with one another.

it is by discourse that men associate; and words are imposed according to the apprehension of the vulgar. And therefore the ill and unfit choice of words wonderfully obstructs the understanding.

Idols of the Theatre

Errors resulting from adherence to philosophical dogma.

  • Sophistical
  • Empirical
  • Superstitious

Skepticism and Dogmatism

I am now therefore to speak touching hope… For without it the rest tends rather to make men sad (by giving them a worse and meaner opinion of how things as they are than they have now, and making them more fully to feel and know the unhappiness of their own condition)

excess is of two kinds: The first being manifest in those who are ready in deciding; and render sciences dogmatic and magisterial; the other in those who deny that we can know anything, and so introduce a wandering kind of inquiry that leads to nothing

The New Organon

Method

The doctrine of those wo have denied that certainty could be attained at all, has some agreement with my way of proceeding [but] the holders of that doctrine … destroy the authority of the senses and understanding; whereas I proceed to devise and supply helps for the same

A New Start

There remains but one course for the recovery of a sound and healthy condition,—namely that the entire work of the understanding be committed afresh, and the mind itself from the very outset not be left to take its own course, but guided at every step; and the business be done as if by machinery

Tabulation

Men of learning… have taken for the construction or the confirmation of their philosophy certain rumours or vague fames… and just as if some kingdom or state were to conduct its affairs… by the gossip of the streets; such exactly is the relation introduced into philosophy with relation to experience.

since there is so great a number and army of particulars… little is to be hoped for … unless all of the particulars which pertain to the subject of inquiry shall, by means of Tables of Discovery, apt, well-arranged, and as it were animate, be drawn up and Marshalled.

Experiment

the best demonstration by far is experience, if it not go beyond the actual experiment.

Induction Vs. Deduction

  1. Customary method—deductive: attempt to begin from highly general truths, and infer many particular facts

  2. Alternative: First particulars, then “middle axioms”, then highly general truths.

The understanding must not therefore be supplied with wings, but hung with weights, to keep it from leaping and flying

The Aim of Science

The Improvement of the human condition.

Not:

  1. Glory, or use in a lecture
  2. In search of natural beauty
  3. Intellectual satisfaction
